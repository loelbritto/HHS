Exploring the impact of ALS on the muscular system and the technological solutions that enabled a brilliant mind to continue changing science

Dr. Stephen Hawking

1942 - 2018

Theoretical Physicist

Click to learn more

Quick Facts

  • Ph.D. from Cambridge University
  • Author of "A Brief History of Time"
  • Presidential Medal of Freedom
  • Lived with ALS for over 50 years
  • Made groundbreaking discoveries about black holes

Who Was Stephen Hawking?

Stephen Hawking was a brilliant theoretical physicist who became one of the most respected and well-known scientists in the world. Despite being diagnosed with ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis) at the age of 21 and given just two years to live, he defied medical expectations and lived for more than five decades with the condition.

His extraordinary mind and determination led to revolutionary contributions to our understanding of black holes, quantum gravity, and the origins of the universe.

ALS and the Muscular System

1963

Diagnosis at Age 21

While studying at Cambridge University, Hawking was diagnosed with Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS), a neurodegenerative disease affecting motor neurons in the brain and spinal cord.

1960s

Initial Symptoms

The disease initially caused muscle weakness, affecting Hawking's ability to walk and perform fine motor tasks. These symptoms resulted from the gradual death of motor neurons that control muscle movement.

1970s

Progressive Deterioration

As ALS progressed, muscle atrophy became more pronounced. Hawking began using a wheelchair as his lower limbs lost strength and function due to lack of neural stimulation of muscle fibers.

1985

Loss of Speech

After developing pneumonia, Hawking underwent a tracheotomy that removed his ability to speak. This procedure was necessary due to weakening respiratory muscles, a common complication of ALS.

Later Years

Almost Complete Paralysis

In his later years, Hawking had extremely limited muscle function, retaining only the ability to control a small facial muscle and some finger movement. This demonstrates how ALS can affect almost all voluntary muscles over time.

Technological Solutions

Speech Synthesizer

Speech Synthesizer

After losing his ability to speak, Hawking initially used a hand-held clicker to select words from a card. Later, he was equipped with a computer-based speech synthesizer that could be controlled with minimal muscle movement.

The iconic voice from this system became synonymous with Hawking's public identity.

Infrared Switch & Cheek Sensor

As Hawking's condition progressed, he adopted an infrared switch mounted on his glasses that detected his cheek movements. This ingenious adaptation allowed him to control his communication system with the only muscle over which he maintained reliable control.

Motorized Wheelchair

Hawking used a specialized motorized wheelchair that compensated for his lack of muscle control. This wheelchair was custom-built with adaptive technology and upgraded throughout his life to incorporate more advanced features.

It eventually included integrated computer systems that controlled both his mobility and communication.

Predictive Text Software

Custom software was developed to enhance Hawking's communication speed. This predictive text system could complete words after just a few characters were selected and learn from his patterns of usage.

With this innovation, Hawking could compose about 15 words per minute, despite having control of only a single muscle.

Hawking's Legacy

Dr. Stephen Hawking's life demonstrates the extraordinary potential of the human mind even when the muscular system fails. His ability to continue groundbreaking scientific work despite severe physical limitations has:

  • Inspired Technological Innovation

    The technologies developed to assist Hawking have benefited countless others with muscular and neurological conditions.

  • Advanced Accessibility

    His prominence helped advocate for greater accessibility in education, science, and public spaces.

  • Challenged Perceptions

    Hawking challenged perceptions about physical disability and demonstrated that cognitive ability remains undiminished despite muscular deterioration.

Additional Case Studies

Facioscapulohumeral Muscular Dystrophy (FSHD)

FSHD Genetic Disorder Muscle Weakness

Patient Profile

Name: John Doe (fictional name for privacy)

Age: 19 years

Gender: Male

Family History: Father and paternal grandfather had mild muscle weakness but were never formally diagnosed.

Medical History: No significant illnesses in childhood; played sports actively until age 15.

Presenting Complaints

  • Facial Weakness: Difficulty smiling and closing his eyes fully while sleeping
  • Shoulder Weakness: Difficulty lifting arms above his head, trouble brushing hair, and noticeable scapular winging
  • Leg Weakness: Recently began experiencing difficulty climbing stairs and occasional tripping
  • Fatigue and Mild Lower Back Pain: Due to compensatory muscle use

Clinical Examination

  • Facial Examination: Incomplete eye closure, reduced ability to puff cheeks, and a weak smile
  • Shoulders & Arms: Scapular winging, difficulty raising arms, and weakness in upper arm muscles
  • Lower Body: Mild weakness in hip flexors and quadriceps, with normal reflexes
  • Gait Analysis: Mildly altered due to weakness, but still able to walk independently

Diagnostic Tests

  • Genetic Testing: Confirmed a DUX4 gene mutation on chromosome 4, confirming FSHD
  • Electromyography (EMG): Showed myopathic changes, consistent with muscular dystrophy
  • Muscle MRI: Indicated muscle atrophy and fatty replacement, especially in the shoulders and thighs

Management Plan

  • Physical Therapy: Strength training, stretching, and postural exercises to improve mobility
  • Bracing & Support: Shoulder braces recommended for better posture and movement
  • Pain Management: NSAIDs and occasional physiotherapy for lower back pain
  • Lifestyle Adjustments: Advised to avoid excessive exertion but maintain regular low-impact exercise
  • Regular Monitoring: Yearly check-ups to track muscle weakness and potential respiratory or cardiac involvement

Follow-up (After 22 Years)

  • Still ambulatory and independent, but with slightly more fatigue
  • Increased shoulder weakness, but physical therapy has helped maintain function
  • No significant respiratory or heart issues detected

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

DMD X-linked Disorder Progressive Muscle Weakness

Patient Profile

Age: 12 years

Gender: Male

Family History: Maternal uncle died of the same illness at a young age

Presenting Complaints

  • Painful decayed tooth in the lower right jaw region
  • Repeated falls
  • Fatigue
  • Muscle weakness
  • Inability to climb stairs

Clinical Examination

  • Difficulty in standing, walking, and getting up from sitting position
  • Proximal weakness
  • Calf hypertrophy
  • Hamstring muscle contracture
  • Positive Gower's sign
  • Anterior open bite
  • Left posterior cross bite
  • Enlarged tongue
  • Crowding in lower anteriors
  • Poor oral hygiene status

Diagnostic Tests

  • Serological Analysis:
    • Creatine kinase (CK): 7342 U/L (elevated)
    • Lactate dehydrogenase: 595 μg/dl
    • Alanine transaminase: 124 U/L
  • Electromyography: Myopathic pattern in right vastus lateralis
  • Muscle Biopsy: Positive for alpha, beta, gamma, delta-sarcoglycan and negative for DYS1, DY2, and DYS3

Management Plan

  • Consultation with pediatrician for general and physical health status
  • Daily physiotherapy
  • Steroid therapy
  • Regular assessment for progressive muscle and cardiac/respiratory damage
  • Dental therapy: pulpectomy and oral prophylaxis
  • Periodic recall to prevent further dental complications
